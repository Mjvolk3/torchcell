\subsection*{Problem setup}
We cast multimodal phenotype prediction as supervised learning of a single map from a cell
to its phenotype. A model $\hat f_\theta$ reads a cell graph $G$, an environment
$\varepsilon$, and a perturbation $p$ and predicts a phenotype
$\hat y=\hat f_\theta(G,\varepsilon,p)$, which we fit by minimizing the expected loss over
the data distribution $D$,
\begin{equation}
\hat\Theta=\arg\min_{\Theta}\ \mathbb{E}_{(G,\varepsilon,p,y)\sim D}
\big[\mathcal{L}\big(\hat f_\theta(G,\varepsilon,p),\,y\big)\big].
\label{eq:objective}
\end{equation}
The contribution is the structure of $\hat f_\theta$, which factors into encode, operate,
and decode,
\begin{equation}
\hat f_\theta(G,\varepsilon,p)=\mathcal{R}_\phi\big(\mathcal{T}_\psi(F_\theta(G),p),\,\varepsilon\big),
\label{eq:factor}
\end{equation}
so that abundant entity data trains the encoder $F_\theta$ while the scarce phenotype
labels need only fit the perturbation operator $\mathcal{T}_\psi$ and the decoders
$\mathcal{R}_\phi$. The notation, the data, and each factor are specified below.

\subsection*{Notation}
We fix one notation, chosen to stay compatible with three literatures---machine learning
on graphs, transformers, and constraint-based metabolic modeling---and to avoid the
symbol collisions between them. Calligraphic letters denote spaces and learned operators. We cast the model as a
three-stage framework---encode (ENC), perturb (PERT), decode (DEC), the stage names in
Fig.~\ref{fig:torchcell}c---instantiated for CGT by the encoder $F_\theta$, perturbation
operator $\mathcal{T}_\psi$, and decoder $\mathcal{R}_\phi$ (Table~\ref{tab:notation}):
ENC/PERT/DEC name the concept, $F_\theta/\mathcal{T}_\psi/\mathcal{R}_\phi$ the CGT
implementation. Table~\ref{tab:notation} is the full glossary.

\subsection*{Ontology-grounded schema and knowledge graph}
Every experimental record is typed by a schema $\Sigma$ grounded in the Biolink Model:
each class maps to a Biolink category and each relation to a Biolink predicate. At
ingestion, records are validated programmatically against properties that keep the schema
machine-checkable:
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{acyclicity}---$\Sigma$ is a directed acyclic graph, so type and relation
dependencies have no cycles and ingestion terminates;
\item \emph{Liskov substitutability}---each subtype is usable wherever its supertype is,
so the class hierarchy is sound and a query on a supertype returns its subtypes;
\item \emph{Biolink conformance}---each class and relation resolves to a Biolink
category/predicate, with field- and cardinality-level constraints;
\item \emph{referential integrity}---each typed edge connects declared node types.
\end{itemize}
$\Sigma$ acts as an hourglass whose narrow waist forces heterogeneous sources into one
typed structure. A raw record $x$ is validated and emitted as a typed subgraph $\iota(x)$
into a Neo4j knowledge graph $\mathcal{K}$. Datasets are then queries over a shared
structure: a \emph{data-specific} query returns one instance with its nearest typed
edges, while a \emph{cross-experiment} query returns all instances sharing a structural
entity (e.g.\ a common environment), joining studies through shared schema entities
(Fig.~\ref{fig:torchcell}a). This homogenizes modular literature and public data into
queryable datasets, addressing the data-fragmentation barrier to deep learning in
metabolic engineering (Supplementary Figs.~\ref{fig:data-model}, \ref{fig:conversion-dedup-agg}).

\subsection*{Datasets: a reference acted on by a perturbation}
Each experiment is a tuple $(G,\varepsilon,p,y)$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:torchcell}b). The minimal
\emph{reference} is a genome---a sequence (FASTA) and an annotation (GFF) giving gene positions and sequence
features; this alone is a valid record. Optional annotations add structure over that
genome---gene, regulatory, and protein-interaction networks $\{A^{(k)}\}$, metabolism
(the bipartite graph from $(S,\rho)$), and Gene Ontology---and are optional precisely
because they presuppose the genome. Data factorize as \emph{few references, many
perturbations}: rather than store a genome per strain (the yeast knockout library is one
reference and ${\sim}10^{4\text{--}6}$ deletion strains), we store a shared reference $G$
and a perturbation $p$ over it, while natural isolates or divergent genomes become new
references. An experiment additionally specifies a perturbation to the genome ($p$, the genotype) and
possibly the environment ($\Delta\varepsilon$, as in a drug or tolerance study), together
with a phenotype $y$. The phenotype may be a scalar such as fitness, a vector such as a
gene-level expression or protein-abundance profile, or a tensor such as a set of morphology
traits or metabolite concentrations, and the formulation is agnostic to its tensor rank. A mechanistic,
explainable join/aggregation step merges instances across compatible conditions (e.g.\
across environments, or recasting lethality to fitness), enlarging datasets and shrinking
the decoder's output domain $\mathcal{Y}$ to fewer symbol types
(\suppfig{fig:conversion-dedup-agg}).

\subsection*{Encoded entities}
The cell's molecular parts---DNA, RNA, protein, and small molecules such as metabolites and
drugs---form the universe $\mathcal{U}$ of \emph{encoded entities}: each entity arrives with
a representation $\operatorname{Embed}:\mathcal{U}\to\mathbb{R}^{d}$, supplied by a
self-supervised model (a DNA, RNA, or protein language model, or a small-molecule encoder),
a learnable lookup $\mathbf{E}\in\mathbb{R}^{N\times d}$, or a fixed featurization.
Membership asks only that an entity \emph{can be encoded}, not that it be pretrained. These
encoders represent any candidate molecule rather than only those native to the organism, and
they place similar entities near one another in $\mathbb{R}^{d}$, so the geometry is a usable
prior: entities with similar representations are expected to act similarly on the cell.

The framing is deliberately \emph{reductionist}---a cell is described by its parts---while an
instance is \emph{holistic}, a specific cell being a collection of entities assembled under a
perturbation and an environment whose phenotype is a property of the whole. We hypothesize
that the phenotype of a combination of entities can be estimated from supervised data, and
that it takes far less phenotype data to do so because the entities arrive with knowledge
already baked in: the self-supervised models supply statistical regularities of sequence and
structure, and the genome annotation that defines each entity supplies curated structural
knowledge. The encoder therefore begins from informative representations rather than from
scratch, moving work off the scarce phenotype labels and onto data that is abundant. This
factorization is a response to the limited-data regime; were phenotype data abundant, the
simpler and more general choice would be to learn the cell-to-phenotype map end to end.
Abundant entity data and scarce labels meet at the representation $H$, where we cut the model
so that the labels fit only the operator and the decoder.

A measured information accounting motivates this cut (Fig.~\ref{fig:torchcell}c; \suppnoteref{note:info-accounting}). Both sides are measured with one instrument, the compressed length
$L_C(D)=8\lvert C(s(D))\rvert$ of a corpus $D$ under a fixed serialization $s$ and lossless
compressor $C$. On the entity side we take the public archives in the representation in which
each is actually distributed---nucleotide (NCBI \texttt{nt}), protein (UniProtKB/TrEMBL),
small molecule (PubChem), and structure (wwPDB)---whose sizes are read from HTTP
\texttt{Content-Length}, BLAST metadata, and the wwPDB index without downloading any payload
(\supptab{tab:entity-corpora}); they total ${\sim}10^{13}$ bits. A counting bound
corroborates the compressor: \texttt{nt} holds $4.06\times10^{12}$ nucleotides, which a
four-letter alphabet cannot write in under $8.1\times10^{12}$ bits, and the archive is
distributed in $7.1\times10^{12}$. On the label side, the phenotype corpus we integrate
(\supptab{tab:datasets}) occupies ${\sim}1.4\times10^{9}$ compressed bytes, or
${\sim}10^{10}$ bits---an asymmetry of ${\sim}10^{3}$.

Scale is only half the argument, and the weaker half. An entity representation is a function
of the entity alone, so one sequence informs the encoder for every instance in which that
entity appears; a phenotype record is pinned to a single genotype, environment, and assay, and
constrains the map nowhere else. Nor are these compressed sizes usable supervision: both are
upper bounds on description length (\suppnoteref{note:info-accounting}, Proposition~\ref{prop:codelength}),
and the labels in particular are noisy and strongly redundant---interaction matrices are
approximately low rank---which places the effective supervision nearer $10^{6}$--$10^{7}$ bits
than $10^{10}$. That discount only sharpens the conclusion. A sequence encoder carries
$10^{8}$--$10^{10}$ parameters and the phenotype labels cannot pay for it, whereas the operator
and decoder together carry ${\sim}5\times10^{4}$ and they can. This is exactly the
encode/operate/decode cut.

\subsection*{Multimodal cell representation}
A cell is represented across nested biological scales (Fig.~\ref{fig:torchcell}d). The
genome is a sequence, the gene networks form a multi-graph with $K$ relation types and
adjacencies $\{A^{(k)}\}$, metabolism is the bipartite gene--reaction graph induced by
$(S,\rho)$, and the environment is a multi-set of conditions. Each gene $g_i$ receives a
node feature $x_i=\operatorname{Embed}(g_i)\in\mathbb{R}^{d}$ from its sequence and
modality embeddings, and a learnable whole-cell token $x_{\mathrm{CLS}}$ is prepended to
form the input states
\begin{equation}
H^{(0)}=(x_{\mathrm{CLS}},x_1,\dots,x_N)\in\mathbb{R}^{(N+1)\times d}.
\label{eq:input}
\end{equation}
The relation adjacencies $\{A^{(k)}\}$ are not consumed as message-passing edges. They
enter the model only as priors on attention (Eq.~\ref{eq:graphreg}), so the encoder reads
the cell as a set of gene tokens whose interactions are learned rather than fixed in
advance.

\subsection*{Cell Graph Transformer: forward pass}
The encoder $F_\theta$ maps the input states $H^{(0)}$ to the cell representation
$H=H^{(L)}$ through $L$ transformer layers (Fig.~\ref{fig:torchcell}e). Layer $\ell$ updates
every token with multi-head self-attention followed by a position-wise feed-forward network. For head
$a\in[h]$ of width $d_k=d/h$, the layer forms queries, keys, and values
\begin{equation}
Q^{(\ell,a)}=H^{(\ell-1)}W_Q^{(\ell,a)},\qquad
K^{(\ell,a)}=H^{(\ell-1)}W_K^{(\ell,a)},\qquad
V^{(\ell,a)}=H^{(\ell-1)}W_V^{(\ell,a)},
\label{eq:qkv}
\end{equation}
and computes an attention map and head output
\begin{equation}
\alpha^{(\ell,a)}=\operatorname{softmax}\!\Big(\frac{Q^{(\ell,a)}\,(K^{(\ell,a)})^{\!\top}}{\sqrt{d_k}}\Big),
\qquad
O^{(\ell,a)}=\alpha^{(\ell,a)}V^{(\ell,a)}.
\label{eq:attn}
\end{equation}
The heads are concatenated and mixed, then combined with the feed-forward network through
residual connections,
\begin{equation}
\hat H^{(\ell)}=H^{(\ell-1)}+\big[\,O^{(\ell,1)}\,\Vert\cdots\Vert\,O^{(\ell,h)}\,\big]W_O^{(\ell)},
\qquad
H^{(\ell)}=\hat H^{(\ell)}+\operatorname{FFN}^{(\ell)}\!\big(\operatorname{LN}(\hat H^{(\ell)})\big),
\label{eq:layer}
\end{equation}
with layer normalization applied before each sublayer. During training, selected heads are
aligned to biological graphs (Eq.~\ref{eq:graphreg}), and this is the only place the
networks $\{A^{(k)}\}$ act on the model. Because the wildtype cell graph $G$ is shared
across the many strains built on it, the encoder runs once per reference,
\begin{equation}
H=F_\theta(G)=(h_{\mathrm{CLS}},h_1,\dots,h_N),
\label{eq:encode}
\end{equation}
and every perturbation reuses this $H$. The reparametrization replaces a per-strain
re-encoding of perturbed graphs, of cost $\mathcal{O}(B\,L\,\lvert E\rvert)$, with one
wildtype encode of cost $\mathcal{O}(B\,L)$ followed by the cheap operator below.

\subsection*{Perturbation operator}
A strain is the reference acted on by a perturbation, and we realize this as a
transformation within embedding space rather than a re-encoding. The operator
$\mathcal{T}_\psi$ applies the perturbation set $p=\{(e_t,\tau_t,m_t)\}_{t=1}^{M}$ by
cross-attention, in which every cell token is a query and the perturbation tokens are the
keys and values. Each perturbation token is embedded as
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{p}_t=r(e_t)+\mathbf{t}_{\tau_t}+m_t\,\mathbf{w},
\qquad
r(e_t)=
\begin{cases}
h_j & \text{if } e_t=g_j \text{ is an in-cell gene,}\\
\operatorname{Embed}(e_t) & \text{otherwise,}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:ptoken}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{t}_{\tau}$ is a learned type embedding that carries the sign of the
perturbation and $\mathbf{w}$ scales it by the magnitude. With $\mathbf{P}=(\mathbf{p}_1,\dots,\mathbf{p}_M)$, every
gene attends over the perturbation context and is updated by a weighted blend of the
values,
\begin{equation}
\beta_{i,t}=\operatorname*{softmax}_{t\in[M]}\frac{(h_iW_Q)\cdot(\mathbf{p}_tW_K)}{\sqrt{d_k}},
\qquad
\Delta h_i=\sum_{t=1}^{M}\beta_{i,t}\,(\mathbf{p}_tW_V).
\label{eq:pert}
\end{equation}
A residual block then produces the perturbed states,
\begin{equation}
\hat h_i=\operatorname{LN}(h_i+\Delta h_i),
\qquad
h_i^{\mathrm{pert}}=\operatorname{LN}\!\big(\hat h_i+\operatorname{FFN}(\hat h_i)\big),
\qquad
H_{\mathrm{pert}}=\mathcal{T}_\psi(H,p).
\label{eq:pertblock}
\end{equation}
The update is a soft blend over the whole set, since $\sum_t\beta_{i,t}=1$, and is not a
dictionary lookup. Because $p$ is a set, the operator is permutation-invariant in the
perturbation tokens and accepts any number of them, and it is equivariant in the genes, so
permuting genes permutes $H_{\mathrm{pert}}$ in step. The same operator therefore extends
from gene deletions to drug, inhibitor, and environment perturbations by widening $p$,
with no change to the architecture (Fig.~\ref{fig:torchcell}f).

Two advantages follow from realizing a strain as an operator rather than a graph. First,
no graph is constructed per strain: the wildtype is encoded once (Eq.~\ref{eq:encode}) and
each of the ${\sim}10^{4\text{--}6}$ strains is obtained by applying $\mathcal{T}_\psi$ to
the shared $H$, so the data pipeline never builds or re-encodes a perturbed graph, and the
operator is a learned, functionally equivalent substitute for re-encoding---applying it to
the reference representation approximates re-encoding the perturbed graph with a graph
network, $\Phi(G_p)\approx\mathcal{R}_\phi(\mathcal{T}_\psi(H,p))$
(Fig.~\ref{fig:torchcell}g; proved in \suppnoteref{note:equivalence})---at
$\mathcal{O}(BL)$ rather than $\mathcal{O}(BL\lvert E\rvert)$ cost. Second, the model does
not require a graph at all: genes enter as a token set and interact through learned
attention, with the networks $\{A^{(k)}\}$ acting only as an optional soft prior
(Eq.~\ref{eq:graphreg}), never as a message-passing substrate. Its minimal input is
therefore a genome---a set of encodable entities---so interaction networks sharpen the
model where available but are not a prerequisite, which lowers the minimal data criterion
for a new organism or dataset.

\subsection*{Multitask readouts}
Each phenotype is read out of the same perturbed representation by a task head
$\mathcal{R}_\phi^{t}$, so one latent state serves every task and the heads differ only in
how they pool over genes. Invariant heads predict a whole-cell scalar. The fitness head
pools all genes,
\begin{equation}
\hat y_{\mathrm{fit}}=\operatorname{MLP}_{\mathrm{fit}}\!\Big(\tfrac{1}{N}\textstyle\sum_{i=1}^{N}h_i^{\mathrm{pert}}\Big),
\label{eq:fit}
\end{equation}
and the genetic-interaction head pools the perturbed genes and compares them against the
whole-cell token,
\begin{equation}
\hat y_{\mathrm{int}}=\operatorname{MLP}_{\mathrm{int}}\!\Big(\big[\,h_{\mathrm{CLS}}^{\mathrm{pert}}\,\big\Vert\,\tfrac{1}{\lvert p\rvert}\textstyle\sum_{g_j\in p}h_j^{\mathrm{pert}}\,\big]\Big).
\label{eq:int}
\end{equation}
Equivariant heads predict one value per gene, as for transcript or protein abundance,
\begin{equation}
\hat y_{\mathrm{expr},i}=\operatorname{MLP}_{\mathrm{expr}}\!\big(h_i^{\mathrm{pert}}\big),
\label{eq:expr}
\end{equation}
and gene-set heads pool over a fixed set $\mathcal{G}_s$ of genes, as for the morphology
traits of a cellular structure,
\begin{equation}
\hat y_{\mathrm{morph}}=\operatorname{MLP}_{\mathrm{morph}}\!\Big(\textstyle\frac{1}{\lvert\mathcal{G}_s\rvert}\sum_{i\in\mathcal{G}_s}h_i^{\mathrm{pert}}\Big).
\label{eq:morph}
\end{equation}
The environment $\varepsilon$ conditions every head, and together the encoder, operator,
and heads realize the predictor $\hat y$ of Eq.~\eqref{eq:objective}. Adding a phenotype is
adding a head, not changing the backbone.

\subsection*{Graph-regularized attention and the training objective}
A biological graph and an attention map are objects of the same shape
(Fig.~\ref{fig:torchcell}h). The adjacency
$A^{(k)}\in\{0,1\}^{N\times N}$ over genes matches the gene--gene block of an attention map
$\alpha^{(\ell,a)}\in[0,1]^{N\times N}$, so the two can be compared row by row. We
row-normalize each adjacency into a distribution over neighbors,
\begin{equation}
\tilde{A}^{(k)}=(D^{(k)})^{-1}A^{(k)},\qquad
D^{(k)}=\operatorname{diag}\!\big(A^{(k)}\mathbf{1}\big),
\label{eq:rownorm}
\end{equation}
and align a chosen head to graph $k$ by penalizing the divergence of each row,
\begin{equation}
\Omega_k=\sum_{i\in\mathcal{I}_k}D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\big(\tilde{A}^{(k)}_{i,:}\,\big\|\,\alpha^{(\ell_k,a_k)}_{i,:}\big),
\label{eq:graphreg}
\end{equation}
where $(\ell_k,a_k)$ is the layer and head assigned to graph $k$ and $\mathcal{I}_k$ is a
sampled set of rows. The supervised term is a multitask loss over a batch of $B$ instances.
The tasks $t$ index the phenotype heads and $b$ indexes the instances in the batch. Most
instances carry a label for only some tasks, so a mask $m_t^{(b)}\in\{0,1\}$, equal to $1$
when the label $y_t^{(b)}$ is measured and $0$ otherwise, restricts each term to the labels
that exist,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{y}=\sum_{t}w_t\sum_{b=1}^{B}m_t^{(b)}\,\ell_t\!\big(\hat y_t^{(b)},y_t^{(b)}\big),
\label{eq:suploss}
\end{equation}
with task weights $w_t$. The full objective adds the graph priors to this supervised loss,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}=\mathcal{L}_{y}+\sum_{k=1}^{K}\lambda_k\,\Omega_k.
\label{eq:loss}
\end{equation}
Using the graphs as a soft prior rather than a hard graph-convolutional layer is
deliberate. An edge with $A^{(k)}_{ij}=1$ carries experimental evidence and is trusted, but
$A^{(k)}_{ij}=0$ usually means the pair has not been tested rather than that no interaction
exists. A hard operator would commit to those uncertain zeros, whereas the KL prior only
pulls attention toward the known edges and lets the data overrule it where the labels
demand. Formally, this soft prior is the relaxation of masked graph-attention: a
graph-attention layer confines each gene to its neighbors through an additive $-\infty$
mask on non-edges, and $\lambda\!\to\!\infty$ drives the regularized self-attention onto that
same neighborhood, so the encoder is functionally equivalent to a graph-attention network
while its forward pass stays mask-free.  rather than masking is the right choice
here because the graph is invariant across perturbations: a per-example mask re-imposes the
same static adjacency on every forward pass and forces a rebuilt graph for each perturbed
strain, whereas a constraint constant across examples is more naturally imposed once, in the
objective, with the perturbation carried by the operator. Two further benefits follow. The same graphs can later be promoted from priors to
prediction targets, and a head that keeps strong weight on a non-edge, where
$A^{(k)}_{ij}=0$ yet $\alpha_{ij}$ stays large, flags a candidate interaction that the
networks have not yet recorded. Only gene--gene graphs are regularized. In the trigenic
experiment these are the $K=9$ physical, regulatory, and TFLink networks together with six
STRING channels, one graph per head. Metabolism is bipartite and has no $N\times N$
adjacency, so it enters as a representation annotation and is never used as an attention
prior.
