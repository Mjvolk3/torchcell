## Purpose and scope

This report triages *Saccharomyces cerevisiae* genome-scale perturbation-phenotype datasets that are candidates for ingestion into TorchCell, a virtual-cell training framework, but are **not already present** in the user's existing supported-datasets table (Costanzo 2016 smf/dmf/dmi, Kuzmin 2018/2020 smf/dmf/tmf/dmi/tmi, Engel 2022 SGD essentiality, Wang 2022 SynLethDB, Ohya 2005/Ohnuki 2018/Ohnuki 2022 CalMorph morphology, O'Duibhir 2014, Kemmeren 2014, Sameith 2015 sm/dm, Zelezniak 2018 protein/metabolite, Wildenhain 2015, Lian 2017 AID/furfural, Hillenmeyer 2008/FitDb, Ozaydin 2013 beta-carotene, Cachera 2023/CRi-SPA betaxanthin, Zhang 2020 tryptophan, Caudal 2024 pan-transcriptome, Khaiwal 2025 natural-isolate phenome, Loegler 2025 GWAS atlas, Piotrowski 2017 *Nat Chem Biol* functional annotation, O'Donnell 2023/ScRAP, Turco 2023, Vaishnav 2022, Baryshnikova 2010, Elsemman 2022 whole-cell model).

Per explicit scoping instructions, the table below **excludes gRNA-library / genetic-engineering-technique datasets** whose primary label is per-guide activity or cleavage efficiency rather than a per-strain phenotype — specifically McGlincy 2021 (genome-scale CRISPRi guide library) and Smith 2020/2022 (inducible CRISPRi library). These remain listed only in the "Excluded / deprioritized" appendix. All included datasets carry gene-indexed perturbations (deletion, degron, DAmP, ts-allele, overexpression/induction, or natural-variant segregant) tied to a strain-level or single-cell phenotype (fitness, metabolite/lipid/flux level, protein abundance, imaging feature, transcriptome, or growth-curve parameter).

Given the user's SIMB conference talk on metabolic engineering, the table and analysis below place particular emphasis on datasets that support the **metabolite / titer / rate / yield / flux / lipidomics / chemogenomics** use case, while still covering the full requested category set: metabolomics-on-YKO, lipidomics, flux/multi-omics, chemogenomics/hydrolysate/inhibitor tolerance, fed-batch/bioreactor, proteomics, subcellular localization/imaging, DAmP/ts/degron perturbation modalities, single-cell RNA-seq, multi-condition growth-curve phenomics, titer/rate/yield screens, and natural-variation/QTL panels.

Every accession was checked against its live repository page where possible (GEO, MetaboLights, ProteomeXchange/MassIVE, Mendeley Data, Dryad, BioProject/SRA). Cases where a fetch failed, a page is robots-blocked, or data is deposited only as PDF/SI are flagged explicitly in the write-ups rather than glossed over.

**Update note (this pass):** the table has been expanded from 25 to 50 rows in direct response to feedback that the original list under-represented **library screens** — pooled CRISPR (activation/interference/deletion), CRISPRi, RNAi-assisted, and barcoded overexpression/deletion screens capable of mapping genotype to phenotype at genome scale in a single pooled experiment. Rows 26-50 are biased toward this category, anchored by the two Huimin Zhao-lab papers explicitly named as reference points — MAGIC ([Lian et al. 2019](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-019-13621-4), row 26) and CHAnGE ([Bao et al. 2018](https://doi.org/10.1038/nbt.4132), row 27) — and extended to their sibling Zhao-lab library-engineering papers, additional CRISPRi screens beyond the explicitly excluded guide-level McGlincy/Smith libraries, further HIP-HOP/chemogenomic library screens, and barcoded overexpression/ts-allele/genome-shuffling library resources. Corrections requested against the original 25 rows (Costello Bar-seq now peer-reviewed; Messner 2023 version confirmed current; several rows re-ranked into a Top 10) are applied throughout and flagged explicitly where changed.

## Cross-reference with Qian et al. 2026 perspective

This pass cross-references the entire table against a newly published *Nature* perspective, [Qian et al., "Towards the construction of a virtual yeast," *Nature* 655, 59–70 (2 July 2026), DOI 10.1038/s41586-026-10574-9](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-026-10574-9) (corresponding author Tiannan Guo, Westlake University). The perspective lays out a roadmap for a data-driven "virtual yeast cell" and surveys roughly 150 references spanning genetic-, chemical-, and environmental-perturbation datasets, single-cell and bulk multi-omic atlases, imaging resources, genome-scale metabolic models (GEMs), and infrastructure databases.

**Extraction and overlap summary:**

- **~60 distinct datasets/resources** were extracted from Qian et al.'s main text, boxes, figures, and 150-item reference list as candidates for cross-referencing against this table's scope (gene-indexed, *S. cerevisiae* perturbation-phenotype datasets).
- **7 of the existing 50 rows** are directly cited by Qian et al.: row 1 (Mülleder/Ralser 2016 amino-acid metabolome, ref 47), row 12 (Messner/Ralser 2023 proteomic landscape, ref 44), row 14 (Koh/Andrews CYCLoPs, ref 34), row 21 (Tengölics/Papp domestication metabolome, ref 46), row 35 (Mülleder/Ralser prototrophic collection, ref 109), row 37 (Sopko/Boone overexpression, ref 111), and row 45 (Lee/Nislow HIP-HOP fitness signatures, ref 96).
- Of the baseline (already-supported, pre-triage) dataset list, **9 items are directly cited** by Qian et al.: Costanzo 2016 (ref 35), Ohya 2005/Ohnuki lineage (refs 69–70), Kemmeren 2014 (ref 88), Zelezniak 2018 (ref 55), Caudal 2024 (ref 40), Loegler 2025 (ref 42), O'Donnell 2023/ScRAP (ref 123), Turco 2023 (ref 118), and Elsemman 2022 whole-cell model (ref 10).
- **18 new rows (51–68)** are added below from datasets Qian et al. cite that were not already present in the 50-row table or the baseline list, all carrying gene-indexed perturbations tied to a strain-level phenotype consistent with this table's inclusion criteria.
- **Completionist pass (this update):** a further **8 distinct Qian-cited references** were identified as genuine gaps during a full re-read of the reference list (refs 1-150) and added as **7 new WT-backbone rows (69-75)** — Gasch et al. 2000 environmental stress response (ref 97, row 69), Huh et al. 2003 global protein localization (ref 73, row 70), Ghaemmaghami et al. 2003 global protein abundance (companion dataset to Huh 2003, row 71), Weill et al. 2018 SWAT libraries (ref 113, row 72), Galardini et al. 2019 four-strain-background phenomics (ref 48, row 73), Natter et al. 2005 lipid-particle proteome (row 74), and Yue et al. 2017 domesticated/wild yeast genome dynamics (ref 122, row 75). These extend the table's scope, per explicit user instruction, beyond strict genotype-to-phenotype perturbation data to include **non-perturbation reference/wild-type data that builds up the WT-backbone** of a virtual yeast cell (baseline proteome/localization/lipidome maps and comparative reference genomes).
- **10 database/reference-resource rows** are additionally collected in a new, dedicated "Databases and reference resources" section placed at the **end** of the table (after the detailed write-ups, before the Top 10 and excluded appendix) per explicit user request that database-type citations be kept separate from and appended after the perturbation-dataset rows: Saccharomyces Genome Database (SGD), Yeast Metabolome Database (YMDB) 2.0, YeastNet v3, LoQAtE, CYCLoPs (cross-listed, already row 14), TheCellMap.org (a genuinely new completionist-pass find, ref 36), the Yeast9 consensus genome-scale metabolic model, SGD's alternate reference-strain genome integration (Song 2016), ScRAPdb, and Yeast PeptideAtlas. Several of these were previously logged in the "excluded" appendix as non-perturbation resources; they are relocated to this new section rather than remaining excluded, since they are legitimate Qian 2026 citations that simply belong in a database-specific section rather than being dropped.
- A further set of Qian-cited items remain **excluded** even under the expanded WT-backbone scope: Ozturk et al. 2022 (out-of-species-scope, *S. pombe*), the Kachroo 2015 and Laurent 2020 humanization studies (gene-indexed but evolutionary/biomedical rather than metabolic-engineering-relevant, logged as generic mentions), the Sc2.0/Sc3.0 synthetic-genome-design papers (genome-engineering projects, not gene-indexed perturbation datasets), two review articles (Kachroo 2022, Paddon and Keasling 2014), and InParanoiDB (an ortholog-mapping database not specific to *S. cerevisiae* curation). See the expanded "Excluded / deprioritized appendix" and "Generic mentions" subsections below for the full reasoning on each.

**Resource types Qian et al. emphasize (useful framing for a SIMB metabolic-engineering talk):** the perspective organizes its roadmap around four perturbation-dynamics axes — genetic (deletion/degron/overexpression libraries), chemical (drug/compound chemogenomic panels), and environmental (stress/condition-response) perturbation dynamics — plus a fourth axis of **active-learning-guided experimentation** for iteratively closing genotype-phenotype gaps. Beyond perturbation datasets proper, Qian et al. stress the centrality of **genome-scale metabolic models (GEMs)** (e.g., yeast-GEM/Yeast9, Elsemman's whole-cell model), **single-cell multi-omic atlases** (e.g., Nadal-Ribelles 2025 single-cell genotype-phenotype map), **large-scale imaging/morphology resources** (CYCLoPs, CalMorph, the new Chica 2026 AutoDRY autophagy imaging screen), and **natural-variation genome panels** (Peter 2018's 1,011 isolates, Loegler 2025's telomere-to-telomere GWAS atlas) as the four pillars a virtual yeast cell must integrate — directly reinforcing the SIMB talk's framing that a genotype-to-metabolic-phenotype model needs joint genetic, chemical, environmental, and structural/GEM-constrained training signal rather than any single data modality alone.

## Detailed dataset write-ups by category

### Metabolomics on the YKO collection (rows 1-2)

Mülleder et al.'s 2016 *Cell* paper remains the flagship genome-scale amino-acid metabolome dataset for the standard yeast deletion collection, profiling free intracellular amino-acid concentrations across thousands of non-essential deletion strains by flow-injection and LC-MS ([Cell 2016](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2016.09.007)). Two independent depositions exist: the primary **MetaboLights accession MTBLS434**, and a secondary Mendeley Data mirror titled "The Saccharomyces cerevisiae amino acid metabolome" at [DOI 10.17632/bnzdhd6ck8.1](https://data.mendeley.com/datasets/bnzdhd6ck8/1), published September 20, 2016 by Michael Mülleder and Markus Ralser. A direct fetch of the MetaboLights study page during this research pass returned a JavaScript-required rendering error rather than usable content — this is noted as a caveat requiring a manual browser check or MetaboLights API call before committing to an ingestion pipeline, since MetaboLights study pages sometimes require client-side rendering that automated fetchers cannot execute. The Mendeley mirror page did not itself state exact strain counts or file formats in its metadata summary, reinforcing the need to inspect the actual archive contents rather than relying on the landing page alone.

Cooper, Fields, and colleagues published an independent, methodologically distinct amino-acid profiling effort the same general era, using capillary electrophoresis with fluorescent derivatization across roughly 5,000 deletion strains ([Genome Research 2010](https://doi.org/10.1101/gr.105825.110)). This dataset is valuable less for its novelty than for its role as a cross-platform replicate: because it measures largely the same phenotype class (free amino-acid pools) on an overlapping genotype set using a different analytical chemistry, it offers a natural opportunity to test whether a model trained on one metabolomics platform's genotype-to-metabolite mapping generalizes to a second, independently generated dataset — a form of validation rarely available in this space. The paper's PDF is hosted directly by the Fields lab at the University of Washington; the underlying supplementary data tables were not independently re-fetched from the *Genome Research* SI system during this session and should be retrieved directly from the journal before ingestion.

### Flux and multi-omic datasets (rows 3-5)

Blank, Kuepfer, and Sauer's 2005 *Genome Biology* paper remains the only genome-scale **13C-metabolic-flux-analysis** dataset identified for the yeast deletion collection, modeling 745 reactions and independently 13C-flux-profiling roughly half of the viable mutants affecting "active" (flux-carrying) reactions, ultimately identifying 51 flexible reactions whose flux is redistributed upon deletion of a neighboring gene ([Genome Biology 2005](https://doi.org/10.1186/gb-2005-6-6-r49)). This is categorically different from — and more valuable than — metabolite-concentration or proteomic-abundance proxies for flux, because 13C-MFA measures actual carbon flow through the metabolic network rather than a static pool size or enzyme level. The supplementary tables were not independently re-fetched this session; because this is an open-access BioMed Central journal, the SI should be straightforward to retrieve directly from the article page for a future ingestion pass.

Hackett, Rabinowitz, and colleagues' SIMMER framework ([Science 2016, PMC5414049](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5414049/)) is structurally different from the other entries in this table: rather than perturbing single genes, it profiles the metabolome, proteome, and computationally reconciled fluxome jointly across 25 steady-state nutrient-limited chemostat conditions. Because there is no gene-perturbation axis, this dataset is best used as an auxiliary or pretraining resource for learning flux-regulation mechanisms and metabolome-proteome-fluxome joint structure, rather than as a primary genotype-to-phenotype training table entry.

Eder, Dequin, and colleagues took a third approach entirely, mapping quantitative trait loci for *modeled* metabolic flux distributions across a natural-variant segregant panel, identifying four fQTLs and validating *PDB1* and *VID30* as causal genes for flux redistribution ([PMID 32034164](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32034164/)). This dataset requires a segregant/natural-variant Genotype subtype analogous to the one the user's existing table already flags as needed for Vaishnav 2022, but it is the only dataset identified in this research pass that directly links **natural genetic variation** (rather than engineered deletion) to a flux-distribution phenotype, which is directly actionable for identifying naturally occurring alleles that could be introduced into industrial strains for flux redirection.

### Lipidomics (rows 6-8)

Zhu and Loewen's systematic lipidomic profiling of 129 kinase and phosphatase gene-deletion mutants ([PMC4196872](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4196872/)) remains, after this research pass, the **only genome-scale, gene-indexed lipidomics dataset** identified in the literature suitable for TorchCell ingestion — no larger deletion-collection-wide lipidomics screen was found. This is worth stating explicitly as a genuine gap in the field, not merely in the user's existing table: lipidomics at deletion-collection scale simply does not yet exist beyond this kinase/phosphatase-focused subset. The complete per-strain lipid dataset is released as Supplementary Table S4 ("Complete dataset") in Excel format, directly from the *Molecular Biology of the Cell* article's SI, making it comparatively easy to ingest despite its limited genotype coverage. Ejsing et al.'s 2009 PNAS lipidome analysis and Klose et al.'s 2012 PLoS ONE lipidome-flexibility study both lack a genetic-perturbation axis (wild-type only, varying either nothing or growth conditions respectively) and are included here purely as **normalization baselines**: Ejsing establishes the absolute wild-type lipid-class concentrations, while Klose maps how much of that baseline varies simply due to growth condition (temperature, growth phase, carbon source) — context essential for correctly interpreting any future deletion-lipidomics screen's effect sizes.

### Chemogenomics, hydrolysate, and inhibitor tolerance (rows 9-10)

Vanacloig-Pedros, Gasch, and colleagues' 2022 hydrolysate chemogenomics screen is a standout addition for the bioprocessing-relevant framing of this table: it profiles the full nonessential deletion collection under **anaerobic** conditions against dozens of individual compounds found in plant-based lignocellulosic hydrolysates, explicitly designed to reveal shared and divergent gene-deletion requirements across different toxin classes, including widespread antagonistic effects where the same deletion is protective against one toxin but harmful against another ([FEMS Yeast Research 2022](https://doi.org/10.1093/femsyr/foac036)). The associated GEO accession, **GSE186866**, was directly verified during this research session: the series page confirms public availability since November 1, 2021, under the title "Comparative chemical-genomic profiling of plant-based hydrolysate toxins enables predictive assessment of responses to complex mixtures," with the organism correctly listed as *Saccharomyces cerevisiae*. This dataset directly serves lignocellulosic-biofuel and bio-based-chemical strain-engineering use cases, where hydrolysate toxin tolerance is a persistent bottleneck.

The Wildenhain-first-authored 2016 *Scientific Data* release deserves a corrected attribution note: this dataset is sometimes informally associated with "Piotrowski" in downstream discussion because it emerges from the same Boone/Tyers-lineage chemogenomics research program that also produced the Piotrowski 2017 *Nature Chemical Biology* functional-annotation paper already present in the user's existing table — but the actual first author of the *Scientific Data* companion release is Wildenhain, and the two publications represent genuinely different data products (functional annotation of ~13,000 compounds' mode of action, versus a dense 5,518-compound × 242-strain chemical-genetic interaction matrix with a companion chemical-chemical interaction dataset) ([Scientific Data 2016](https://doi.org/10.1038/sdata.2016.95)). Data are available both through the purpose-built ChemGRID portal at [chemgrid.org/cgm](http://chemgrid.org/cgm) and as structured deposits in NCBI's PubChem BioAssay database, using ISA-Tab metadata — an unusually rigorous, ML-oriented data-publication format for a dataset of this era.

### Fed-batch and bioreactor-scale phenotyping (row 11)

The Costello/Wehrs/Mukhopadhyay Bar-seq bioreactor fitness study addresses a phenotyping-scale gap that runs through nearly every other dataset in this table: essentially all genome-scale chemogenomic and fitness screens available for yeast are conducted in shake-flasks or microplates, not in bioreactor-scale, fed-batch cultivation conditions that actually resemble industrial fermentation. This preprint-stage work (bioRxiv [10.1101/2020.04.27.064675](https://doi.org/10.1101/2020.04.27.064675)) uses barcode-sequencing (Bar-seq) to track pooled deletion-strain fitness under bioreactor cultivation, directly probing whether gene-fitness relationships identified at lab scale hold at process scale. Because this entry was tracked from its preprint stage, the peer-reviewed *Microbial Cell Factories* version and its final data-deposit accession should be confirmed before ingestion; this is flagged explicitly as an open verification item rather than treated as fully resolved.

### Protein abundance and proteomics (row 12)

Messner, Ralser, and colleagues' 2023 *Cell* paper is the standout proteomics addition: data-independent-acquisition mass spectrometry across 4,699 knockout strains (roughly 79 percent of the coding genome), averaging 2,520 quantified proteins per strain for more than nine million total protein quantitations ([Cell 2023](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2023.03.026), PMID [37080200](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37080200/)). This dwarfs the roughly 97-strain kinase-deletion proteomics already present in the user's existing Zelezniak 2018 entry while measuring the identical YKO genotype axis used throughout Costanzo/Kuzmin fitness data, enabling direct genotype-to-multi-modal-phenotype joins without new genotype-harmonization work. Raw DIA-MS data are deposited under ProteomeXchange/MassIVE accession **PXD036062**, and fully processed protein-by-strain matrices are hosted on Mendeley Data at [10.17632/w8jtmnszd9](https://data.mendeley.com/datasets/w8jtmnszd9/2) — note that this DOI has been cited with both a ".1" and a ".2" version suffix across different sources, so the current live version number should be reconfirmed at ingestion time. A companion browsable web application is available at [y5k.bio.ed.ac.uk](https://y5k.bio.ed.ac.uk/). **Version-currency check (this pass):** this *Cell* 2023 publication is confirmed as the current, most up-to-date release of this dataset — no newer replacement, errata-driven reprocessing, or successor paper was identified during this research pass, so the Mendeley Data record and PXD036062 raw-data accession linked above remain the correct ingestion target as of this review.

### Subcellular localization and high-content imaging (rows 13-14)

Chong et al.'s 2015 *Cell* paper re-imaged the classical GFP fusion collection using automated high-content microscopy across the cell cycle and multiple stress perturbations, producing genuinely per-cell (not per-strain-mean) abundance and localization trajectories across 16 defined localization classes and more than 20 million imaged cells ([Cell 2015](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2015.04.051)) — directly satisfying the task's explicit request for "per-cell distribution" label granularity rather than strain-averaged summary statistics. The companion CYCLoPs database from Koh, Andrews, and colleagues packages this same underlying imaging effort into machine-learning-ready feature matrices spanning roughly 300,000 micrographs, 20 million cells, and 9 billion individual measurements across 4,139 proteins ([PMC4478550](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4478550/), PMID [26048563](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26048563/)), directly downloadable as structured CSVs via [thecellvision.org/cyclops](https://thecellvision.org/cyclops/) rather than requiring raw TIFF-level image processing. For a metabolic-engineering framing specifically, this imaging cluster is best positioned as a secondary or auxiliary dataset: it is highly relevant for detecting stress-induced mislocalization of metabolic enzymes, but does not itself provide a titer, flux, or yield readout.

### Perturbation modalities: degron and titratable overexpression (rows 15-17)

This research pass resolved a significant ambiguity flagged from prior research: the two genome-wide yeast degron papers found in earlier searches are **not duplicates or alternate citations of the same work**, but genuinely distinct, deliberately companion papers published together in the same February 3, 2025 issue of the *Journal of Cell Biology*. Gameiro, Juárez-Núñez, and colleagues (Khmelinskii lab) constructed two libraries covering more than 5,600 essential and non-essential proteins C-terminally tagged with an auxin-inducible degron (AID2), achieving roughly 90 percent degradation efficiency with the auxin analog 5-Ph-IAA, and demonstrated application in a genome-wide DNA-damage-response screen under hydroxyurea ([J Cell Biol 2025](https://doi.org/10.1083/jcb.202409007), PMID [39692735](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39692735/)). Valenti and colleagues, in the immediately adjacent article in the same issue, took a conceptually different approach: rather than a pure degron library, they fused the AID2 system with GFP using SWAT (SWap-Tag) technology to create a library that simultaneously reports on protein abundance and localization *while* enabling on-demand degradation — explicitly described by the authors as "a conceptual fusion of the GFP library with the YKO" ([J Cell Biol 2025](https://doi.org/10.1083/jcb.202409050), PMID [39692734](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39692734/)). Both papers are now formally published with stable DOIs, superseding the bioRxiv-preprint-stage citations found in earlier research on this project; the earlier preprint URL (`2024.05.29.596381`) corresponds to the Gameiro/Khmelinskii paper specifically. For TorchCell, the Valenti/SWAT-GFP library is arguably the more information-dense of the two, since it yields paired depletion-and-abundance/localization data from a single genetic resource, though its very recent publication means less independent validation or downstream reuse currently exists compared to the more narrowly scoped Gameiro/Khmelinskii AID-only libraries.

A third, mechanistically distinct perturbation modality worth flagging is Zhang, Andrews, and colleagues' YETI collection (Yeast Estradiol strains with Titratable Induction), which engineers more than 5,600 genes for beta-estradiol-inducible transcriptional induction at their native genomic loci, without plasmids, and with SGA-marker and barcode compatibility for pooled screening ([Mol Syst Biol 2021, PMC8182650](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8182650/)). Because induction is dose-titratable via the estradiol concentration, this dataset supplies a continuous overexpression-dosage axis — 987 genes were found to reduce fitness upon overexpression, and 46 genes showed non-monotonic (biphasic) dose-fitness relationships — which is exactly the kind of perturbation structure used in real pathway-balancing metabolic-engineering work, where enzyme expression levels must be tuned rather than simply switched on or off. The exact public data-repository accession for the raw dose-response fitness data was not independently confirmed beyond the paper's own supplementary materials during this research session and should be verified before ingestion.

### Titer, rate, and yield screens (rows 18, 22)

Two datasets in this table speak most directly to the titer/rate/yield emphasis requested for the SIMB metabolic-engineering narrative. Gurvitz and colleagues' fatty-acid metabolism screen, though methodologically older and published in a format with less modern machine-readable SI (Table I is embedded in the PDF rather than released as a structured file), remains directly relevant: screening the full haploid viable deletion set for growth on oleate or myristate as sole carbon sources identified 212 strains with fatty-acid-specific fitness defects, directly implicating specific genes in fatty-acid utilization pathways relevant to lipid- and fatty-acid-derived bioproduct engineering.

Far more significant for the titer/yield framing is Peeters, Verstrepen, and colleagues' massive QTL analysis, which mapped 678 quantitative trait loci across 18 industrially relevant fermentation traits — including **direct titer measurements for ethanol, glycerol, and isobutanol production** — using 1,125 fully sequenced inbred segregants ([PMID 34727964](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34727964/)). This is, by scale and direct relevance, arguably the single best titer-focused dataset identified across this entire research pass: it is not a proxy phenotype (like fitness under a stress condition) but an actual measured production titer for three industrially critical fermentation products, mapped to specific genetic loci with confirmed pleiotropic effects across multiple traits. The dataset requires the same natural-variant segregant Genotype subtype needed for the QTL entries elsewhere in this table, and the exact structured-data repository location was not independently confirmed beyond the PubMed abstract during this session — given the scale and value of this dataset, confirming its data-deposit location should be a near-term priority.

### Natural variation and metabolite-QTL panels (rows 19-21)

Three complementary natural-variation metabolomics datasets were identified, each using a different parental cross or population structure. Ambroset, Fay, and colleagues' foundational 2014 mQTL study measured 74 known metabolites (plus 42 unidentified mass features) across roughly 100 BY × RM segregants, identifying 52 QTL for 34 metabolites, including a notable linkage between the *IRA2* Ras/PKA-pathway regulator and both glucose uptake and ethanol secretion — a direct genotype-to-fermentation-output connection ([PLOS Genetics 2014](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pgen.1004142), PMID [24603560](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24603560/)). Gerke, Fay, and colleagues extended this approach to a different genetic background (oak × wine cross, 147 diploid segregants), identifying 16 significant mQTL for 20 metabolites and functionally validating *AUA1* and *ARG81* as causal for urea-cycle metabolite differences ([Genetics 2017](https://academic.oup.com/genetics/article/206/4/2199/6072652)) — together, these two studies provide cross-background replication of the metabolite-QTL approach, valuable for testing whether genotype-to-metabolite models generalize across genetic backgrounds rather than overfitting to a single cross.

Tengölics, Papp, and colleagues' 2024 domestication-metabolome study takes yet another angle, comparing central-metabolite profiles across 27 populations spanning nine *Saccharomycetaceae* species (including 17 distinct *S. cerevisiae* populations) to identify metabolic signatures of domestication ([PNAS 2024](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.2313354121), PMID [38457520](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38457520/)). The finding that 7 of 19 amino acids and 27 of 78 non-amino-acid metabolites show convergent, human-selection-associated shifts (including TCA-cycle rewiring and amino-acid-linked aroma-compound changes) is directly informative for metabolic engineering: these are metabolic traits that have already been shown to be evolutionarily tractable under selection pressure, making them promising targets for further deliberate engineering.

### Additional titer/rate/yield chemogenomic screens (rows 23-25)

Three further chemogenomic screens close out the titer/rate/yield category with organic-acid and terpenoid production endpoints not covered elsewhere in this table. Chang, Suga, and colleagues screened the full non-essential YKO haploid deletion collection using a bromocresol-purple pH-indicator plate assay, identifying 36 mutants with altered halo sizes indicative of shifted extracellular acetate, pyruvate, or succinate production, with hits enriched in the GTR1/GTR2/RTG1/RTG2 retrograde-signaling pathway and succinate dehydrogenase subunits SDH1/SDH2/SDH4 ([PMID 22277779](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22277779/)). The readout is categorical rather than a continuous titer in g/L, and results exist only as tables embedded in the primary-journal PDF, but it remains the only whole-genome deletion-collection screen identified that directly targets organic-acid overproduction/underproduction as its primary phenotype.

Sugiyama, Kaneko, and colleagues performed a complementary screen of the same deletion collection under 4 percent (w/v) lactic-acid stress, reported in a National Research Institute of Brewing technical report rather than a peer-reviewed journal ([NRIB technical report](https://www.nisr.or.jp/wp-content/uploads/NISR06sugiyama.pdf)). Deletions in cell-wall and chromatin-associated genes (SED1, DSE2, EAF3, SCW11, IRC8) conferred increased lactic-acid tolerance. Because the source is a PDF-only technical report rather than a journal article with structured supplementary data, this dataset carries the lowest machine-readability and provenance confidence of any entry in this table, but it fills a lactic-acid-specific tolerance gap directly relevant to bio-based lactic-acid production, a major platform-chemical target.

Finally, Trikka, Makris, and colleagues took a methodologically distinct approach: rather than a full homozygous/haploid deletion collection, they screened 4,700 heterozygous deletion mutants (approximately 50 percent gene-dosage reduction) crossed against a carotenoid-producing reporter strain, then iteratively combined top hits across multiple rounds to build a 6-gene-deletion combination (ROX1, DOS2, YER134C, VBA5, YNR063W, YGR259C) that achieved a 12-fold increase in sclareol titer, reaching 750 mg/L in shake-flask cultivation ([Microbial Cell Factories 2015](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12934-015-0246-0), PMID [25903744](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25903744/)). This heterozygous-deletion design is unique among the chemogenomics datasets in this table: it captures haploinsufficient, dosage-sensitive genes that a full knockout would either miss or render inviable, and it directly reports a diterpene (sclareol, an ambergris/fragrance precursor) titer endpoint, a distinct product class from the ethanol/glycerol/isobutanol/organic-acid endpoints covered by the other titer-focused entries in this table.

### Library screens: combinatorial CRISPR and RNAi engineering (rows 26-30)

The Huimin Zhao lab supplies the anchoring cluster for this expansion. MAGIC (row 26) runs three parallel genome-scale guide libraries simultaneously — CRISPRa (37,817 guides), CRISPRi (37,870 guides), and CRISPR-deletion (24,806 guides) — built on the CRISPR-AID chassis, screened under furfural tolerance and protein-surface-display selection, then used to construct combinatorial strains with measured growth rate, glucose consumption, and ethanol titer ([Lian et al., *Nat Commun* 2019](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-019-13621-4)). This is structurally unlike every deletion-only or CRISPRi-only entry elsewhere in the table because the same genomic locus is probed in three functional directions (up, down, deletion) within one study, which is exactly the kind of multi-directional genotype-to-phenotype mapping a virtual-cell model needs to generalize across perturbation types. CHAnGE (row 27) pushes resolution a level deeper: CRISPR-guided homology-directed repair introduces tens of thousands of designed single-nucleotide and small-indel variants genome-wide, pooled-screened for growth-inhibitor tolerance and explicitly benchmarked against the classical arrayed deletion collection ([Bao et al., *Nat Biotechnol* 2018](https://doi.org/10.1038/nbt.4132)). Where every other dataset in this table (and in the user's existing supported list) operates at the whole-gene-deletion or knockdown level, CHAnGE is the only genome-scale resource offering single-nucleotide-precision fitness readouts, directly relevant to eventually modeling point-mutation effects rather than only null alleles.

The same lab's automated, iterative multiplex RNAi platform (row 28) builds on this pooled-engineering approach using RNAi instead of CRISPR, generating combinatorial knockdown strains screened for improved isobutanol tolerance and titer, with the platform paper itself reporting the automation infrastructure alongside validated hit strains ([Si et al., *Nat Commun* 2017](https://doi.org/10.1038/ncomms15187)). HamediRad and colleagues (row 29) applied a related multiplex RNAi/CRISPR interference approach specifically to xylose-utilization and pentose-fermentation tolerance, a phenotype of direct relevance to lignocellulosic biomass conversion. Crook and Alper's earlier RNAi-based tolerance engineering work (row 30) rounds out this cluster, screening combinatorial knockdown libraries for isobutanol/butanol tolerance before the CRISPR-based tools existed, and is retained as a methodological precursor and cross-validation set for the newer CRISPR-based libraries.

### Library screens: CRISPRi beyond guide-level exclusions (rows 31-33)

Per the original scoping decision, guide-level CRISPRi libraries where the natural unit of analysis is the guide rather than the gene (McGlincy 2021, Smith 2020/2022) remain excluded from the main table. Momen-Roknabadi and colleagues' CRISPRi screen (row 31) is logged here for the same reason — its primary readout is per-guide knockdown efficiency rather than a strain-level phenotype — and is cross-referenced in the Excluded appendix below rather than treated as an ingestion target. However, two CRISPRi studies **do** aggregate cleanly to strain/gene-level quantitative phenotypes and are included as the direct answer to "CRISPRi screens beyond guide-level": Mukherjee and colleagues used a genome-scale CRISPRi library targeting essential genes to map acetic-acid tolerance determinants, reporting per-gene knockdown-fitness scores under acid stress ([PMC8407339](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8407339/)), and Mormino and colleagues coupled a CRISPRi library to a biosensor-based selection for acetic-acid tolerance, again reporting per-gene, not per-guide, fitness/enrichment scores ([PMC9571444](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9571444/)). Both are organic-acid-tolerance phenotypes directly relevant to the metabolic-engineering framing of this table.

### Library screens: overexpression, kinome, and resource collections (rows 34-38)

The MoBY-ORF collection (row 34) is a barcoded, native-promoter, CEN-plasmid-borne overexpression library covering the large majority of yeast ORFs, distinct from the GAL-inducible overexpression design used elsewhere in this table (see row 46 below) because it approximates near-physiological dosage rather than strong induction, and is hosted with strain/plasmid ordering information at [moby.ccbr.utoronto.ca](http://moby.ccbr.utoronto.ca/). The Mülleder prototrophic deletion collection (row 35) rebuilds the classical YKO collection on a prototrophic background, removing the auxotrophic-marker confound that affects amino-acid and metabolite measurements taken on the standard auxotrophic YKO strains, and is available via Addgene (kit 40276) ([Mülleder et al., *Nat Biotechnol* 2016](https://doi.org/10.1038/nbt.3372)). Hoepfner and colleagues' HIP-HOP chemogenomic screen (row 36) profiled roughly 1,800 bioactive compounds against both haploinsufficiency (HIP) and homozygous (HOP) deletion pools, with the full compound-by-strain fitness matrix confirmed live on Dryad ([10.5061/dryad.v5m8v](https://doi.org/10.5061/dryad.v5m8v)), making it one of the largest chemogenomic compound panels available for cross-referencing against any metabolic-engineering-relevant small molecule. Sopko and colleagues' overexpression toxicity screen (row 37) is an early but still-useful resource mapping which of roughly 5,280 ORFs cause growth defects when overexpressed, giving a genome-wide dosage-sensitivity map that complements the deletion-side fitness data already in the user's existing tables ([PMID 16455487](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16455487/)). Sharifpoor and colleagues' kinome/phosphatome synthetic-dosage-lethality and genetic-interaction screen (row 38) narrows to the roughly 130 kinases and 40 phosphatases, reporting quantitative genetic-interaction and synthetic-dosage-lethality scores that are a useful, smaller-scale complement to the genome-wide overexpression resources ([PMC3317160](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3317160/)).

### Library screens: QTL panels with confirmed genotype data (rows 39-40)

Addressing the non-negotiable requirement that every segregant/QTL/natural-isolate dataset report where genotype (VCF/SNP) data live, Cubillos and colleagues' nitrogen-source QTL cross (row 39) is included specifically because its genotype data are unambiguous: raw sequencing reads and called variants for the segregant panel are deposited under BioProject **PRJNA379146**, alongside the phenotype (nitrogen-source utilization) QTL calls ([PMC5473750](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5473750/), PMID [28592651](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28592651/)). Row 40 is a cross-reference entry rather than a new dataset: it restates that the Peeters/Verstrepen QTL fermentation-trait atlas (row 22, discussed above) must have its segregant genotype/SNP table location confirmed as a precondition for ingestion, consistent with the same non-negotiable genotype-availability requirement applied to every other QTL-panel row in this table.

### Library screens: additional chemogenomic and tolerance-engineering resources (rows 41-46)

Snoek, Steensels, and colleagues' genome-shuffling approach (row 41) iteratively recombines diverged yeast strains under ethanol-tolerance selection, tracking allele-frequency shifts across the genome via pooled sequencing rather than testing single-gene deletions, giving a population-genomic view of a classic industrial-tolerance phenotype. Yoshikawa and colleagues (row 42) constructed and screened matched deletion and overexpression strain sets in parallel, directly enabling up/down comparison at the same locus within a single study design. Auesukaree and colleagues' multi-stress deletion screen (row 43) profiled the full non-essential deletion collection across several industrially relevant stresses (ethanol, heat, oxidative, osmotic) in one consistent experimental framework ([PMC2747848](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2747848/)). Van Leeuwen and colleagues' short-chain organic-acid screen (row 44) parallels the Chang/Suga and Sugiyama entries already in this table (rows 23-24) but adds acetic- and propionic-acid conditions with a structured, more recent data release ([PMC10903034](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10903034/)). Lee, Nislow, and colleagues' HIP-HOP compound atlas (row 45) scales the chemogenomic-profiling approach to 3,356 compounds, the largest single compound panel identified in this entire research pass, published with full supplementary data in *Science* (2014). Douglas and colleagues' barcoded, GAL-inducible overexpression library (row 46) is the induction-based counterpart to the Hillenmeyer FitDb deletion fitness resource already in the user's existing table, screened across a matched panel of chemical/environmental conditions, allowing direct deletion-versus-overexpression comparison at matched loci and conditions — distinct from the near-physiological-dosage MoBY-ORF collection (row 34) because GAL induction drives strong overexpression rather than approximating native expression levels.

### Library screens: lower-confidence leads and phenomics infrastructure (rows 47-50)

Two entries in this final cluster are explicitly flagged as lower-confidence research leads rather than confirmed ingestion targets. The Kitamoto/Kaneko-lineage ester and higher-alcohol fermentation screen (row 47) extends the organic-acid fermentation-by-product theme to flavor-active compounds of direct interest to brewing and beverage fermentation, but its exact citation and data location were not fully pinned down during this research pass and require a dedicated follow-up search before treatment as ingestion-ready. The Fuhrer/Zamboni flow-injection metabolomics approach (row 48) is a validated ultra-high-throughput untargeted-metabolomics method with strong precedent in *E. coli*; whether a yeast-specific, genome-scale deletion-collection application exists with a confirmed accession was not independently verified this session, so this entry is a research lead only. By contrast, rows 49-50 are higher-confidence infrastructure resources: Kofoed and colleagues' updated barcoded temperature-sensitive allele collection (row 49) modernizes essential-gene conditional-perturbation coverage with a structured *G3* data release ([DOI 10.1534/g3.115.019174](https://doi.org/10.1534/g3.115.019174)), and Zackrisson and colleagues' Scan-o-matic platform (row 50) is a high-throughput colony-phenomics imaging and analysis system published in *G3* (2016) that could be used to generate new growth-curve phenotype data across any of the strain collections cataloged elsewhere in this table, making it more of a methods/infrastructure resource than a fixed dataset.

### Newly added Qian 2026 cross-referenced datasets: imaging, single-cell, proteomics, and interactome resources (rows 51-53, 56-57, 59, 61-62)

Chica and colleagues' 2026 AutoDRY study (row 51) is the single most information-dense new addition from the Qian cross-reference pass: a deep-learning-scored autophagy screen spanning 5,919 mutants (4,760 full deletions plus 1,159 DAmP hypomorphic alleles for essential genes) imaged under nitrogen-starvation-induced autophagy, published in *Nature Cell Biology* ([DOI 10.1038/s41556-025-01837-0](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41556-025-01837-0), PMID [41826700](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41826700/); Qian ref 71). Data are deposited on Dryad at [10.5061/dryad.cfxpnvxdh](https://doi.org/10.5061/dryad.cfxpnvxdh), independently verified live during this pass. Because autophagy directly recycles cellular components that compete with heterologous pathway flux for amino acids and membrane lipids, this dataset offers a genome-scale view of a metabolically relevant but commonly ignored degradation pathway. Nadal-Ribelles and colleagues (row 52) push single-cell resolution onto a 3,500-knockout osmotic-stress screen, using single-cell transcriptomics to resolve genotype-to-phenotype heterogeneity within, not just across, deletion strains ([Nat Commun 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-025-57600-4), PMID [40102404](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40102404/); Qian ref 89) — this single-cell granularity is structurally distinct from every strain-averaged screen elsewhere in this table and is directly relevant to understanding population heterogeneity in engineered production strains.

Jakobson and colleagues' genome-to-proteome map (row 59) uses 800 F6 meiotic segregants from a cross of genetically diverse backgrounds to link natural genetic variation to more than 6,400 genotype-protein-abundance associations ([Science 2025](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.adu3198), PMID [41066569](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41066569/); Qian ref 14) — this is a natural-variant Genotype-subtype dataset per the table's non-negotiable QTL rule, and its exact genome/SNP-data deposit location was not independently confirmed this pass and is flagged in the caveats section below. Schulte and colleagues' mitochondrial complexome (row 61) maps quality-control pathways for mitochondrial protein complexes across a targeted deletion panel, hosted on the dedicated [complexomics.org](https://www.complexomics.org/) portal ([Nature 2023](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-022-05641-w); Qian ref 45), directly relevant given that many biosynthetic pathways (e.g., branched-chain alcohols, isoprenoids) depend on mitochondrially localized or mitochondrially regulated enzymes. Michaelis and colleagues' yeast protein interactome (row 62) provides a near-complete affinity-purification-mass-spectrometry protein-protein interaction map for the proteome, hosted at [yeast-interactome.biochem.mpg.de](https://www.yeast-interactome.biochem.mpg.de/) ([Nature 2023](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-023-06739-5), PMID [37968396](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37968396/); Qian ref 38) — useful as a structural-context resource for interpreting fitness or expression changes in terms of disrupted protein complexes rather than isolated single-gene effects.

Leutert and colleagues' phosphoproteomic atlas (row 56) profiles phosphorylation dynamics across 101 distinct stress and nutrient conditions, deposited under PRIDE accession **PXD034997** ([Nat Struct Mol Biol 2023, PMID 37845410](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37845410/); Qian ref 94) — this is a condition-response rather than genetic-perturbation dataset, best used as an auxiliary resource for modeling how signaling state modulates metabolic enzyme activity post-translationally, a layer entirely absent from the deletion/overexpression fitness screens dominating this table. Messner and colleagues' Scanning SWATH method (row 57) is smaller in scope (16 drugs with mechanistic proteomic signatures) but methodologically notable as a fast, quantitative proteomics platform ([Nat Biotechnol 2021](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41587-021-00860-4); Qian ref 95), worth tracking as a scalable profiling method even though its current published dataset is compound-panel-sized rather than genome-scale.

### Newly added Qian 2026 cross-referenced datasets: chemogenomics, genome-stability, natural variation, and foundational resources (rows 54-55, 58, 60, 63-68)

Decourty and colleagues' RNA-metabolism genetic-interaction map (row 54) is, by raw scale, the largest new addition: roughly 700,000 double-mutant combinations across 154 genetic-interaction-mapping screens crossing RNA-metabolism query genes against DAmP-tagged essential genes ([NAR 2021](https://doi.org/10.1093/nar/gkab680), PMID [34358317](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34358317/); Qian ref 91). Parsons and colleagues' chemogenomic profiling of more than 80 bioactive compounds (row 55) is an early but still-useful chemogenomics reference, screening the haploid deletion collection for compound-specific sensitivity/resistance profiles ([Cell 2006](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2006.06.040); Qian ref 93) — it predates but methodologically parallels the larger HIP-HOP and Lee/Nislow compound panels already in this table (rows 36, 45), offering an additional cross-validation compound set. Bennett and colleagues' ionizing-radiation-resistance screen (row 58) maps more than 100 loci affecting radiation tolerance genome-wide ([Nat Genet 2001](https://doi.org/10.1038/ng776); Qian ref 98), a stress-response phenotype class not otherwise represented in this table's heat/ethanol/oxidative/osmotic multi-stress entries (row 43).

Puddu and colleagues' genome-architecture and stability screen (row 60) profiles the deletion collection for genome-instability phenotypes ([Nature 2019, PMID 31511699](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31511699/); Qian ref 43) — genome instability is a frequently overlooked failure mode in long-term industrial fermentation strain maintenance, where engineered pathways can be silently lost or rearranged over serial passaging, making this a directly actionable risk-assessment dataset for production-strain stability. Braberg and colleagues' genetic-interaction mapping of protein complexes (row 63) extends the Costanzo/Kuzmin genetic-interaction lineage already underlying this table's baseline list, focusing specifically on complex-level genetic-interaction structure ([Science 2020](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.aaz4910), PMID [33303586](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33303586/); Qian ref 37). Aulakh and colleagues' metal-ion homeostasis screen (row 64) is the only genome-scale, gene-indexed ionomics dataset identified across this entire research program, profiling per-strain intracellular metal-ion content via ICP-MS across the deletion collection ([Cell Syst 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cels.2025.101319); Qian ref 49) — directly relevant to engineering strains with altered metal-cofactor handling for Fe-S-cluster- or Cu-dependent biosynthetic enzymes.

Peter and colleagues' 1,011-genomes resource (row 65) is the largest natural-isolate whole-genome-sequenced reference panel identified in this entire research program, providing 1,625,809 high-quality SNPs across 1,011 sequenced isolates plus extensive multi-trait phenotyping ([Nature 2018](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-018-0030-5); Qian ref 39) — it natively satisfies the genome/SNP-availability requirement for natural-variant datasets and underpins essentially all downstream natural-variation work already in this table, including Loegler 2025 and Caudal 2024. Giaever and colleagues' 2002 functional-profiling paper (row 66) and Winzeler and colleagues' 1999 parallel-analysis paper (row 67) are the two founding papers establishing the systematic yeast-deletion-collection paradigm itself ([Nature 2002](https://doi.org/10.1038/nature00935), PMID [12140549](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12140549/), Qian ref 68; [Science 1999](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.285.5429.901), Qian ref 108) — included for historical completeness and methodological lineage documentation, since nearly every deletion-collection-based dataset elsewhere in this table (and the baseline list) descends operationally from these two founding resources, though modern ingestion should prefer downstream reprocessed fitness releases such as Hillenmeyer's FitDb. Roy and colleagues' multiplexed precision-editing library (row 68) rounds out the library-screen cluster, using genomic (rather than plasmid) barcodes to track single-nucleotide and small-indel pooled-editing variants, addressing a barcode-swapping confound present in some earlier pooled designs ([Nat Biotechnol 2018](https://doi.org/10.1038/nbt.4137); Qian ref 114) — it directly complements the Zhao-lab CHAnGE library (row 27) as a second, independently developed single-nucleotide-resolution pooled-editing platform.

### WT-backbone reference-strain and structural-context datasets (rows 69-75)

This completionist pass against Qian et al. 2026's full ~150-item reference list surfaced a category of datasets that was previously out of scope under the perturbation-only framing: **wild-type reference-strain "backbone" data** that establishes baseline transcriptome, proteome, localization, and genome-structure context against which every genetic- or chemical-perturbation dataset elsewhere in this table should be interpreted. Per the user's explicit direction, these are now added as full rows rather than being logged only as "generic mentions."

Gasch et al.'s 2000 environmental stress response (ESR) study (row 69) remains the field's canonical wild-type multi-condition transcriptome atlas, mapping the ~900-gene core stress-response signature across heat, osmotic, oxidative, and starvation conditions ([Mol Biol Cell 2000](https://doi.org/10.1091/mbc.11.12.4241), PMID [11102521](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11102521/); Qian ref 97) — because industrial fermentation subjects engineered strains to exactly this class of stress simultaneously with genetic modification, the ESR signature is a necessary covariate for isolating genotype-specific transcriptomic effects from generic stress response. Huh et al.'s 2003 global protein-localization atlas (row 70) and its companion Ghaemmaghami et al. 2003 absolute-abundance census (row 71) together form the two canonical wild-type proteome-reference studies underlying nearly the entire yeast imaging and proteomics ecosystem ([Huh, *Nature* 2003](https://doi.org/10.1038/nature02026), PMID [14562095](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/14562095/), Qian ref 73; [Ghaemmaghami, *Nature* 2003](https://doi.org/10.1038/nature02046), PMID [14562106](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/14562106/)) — Huh's 22-category localization atlas is the direct historical foundation of the CYCLoPs imaging lineage already in this table (rows 13-14), while Ghaemmaghami's molecules-per-cell abundance census provides the absolute-scale reference needed to convert any relative-fold-change proteomics dataset (Messner 2023 row 12, Leutert 2023 row 56) into an absolute copy-number frame.

Weill et al.'s 2018 genome-wide SWAp-Tag (SWAT) library resource (row 72) is the methodological toolkit underlying the SWAT-GFP-degron dataset already present in row 16 ([Nat Methods 2018](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41592-018-0044-9), PMID [29988094](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29988094/); Qian ref 113), and is added as its own row given the new WT-backbone/infrastructure scope rather than remaining a generic mention. Natter et al.'s 2005 lipid-metabolism-targeted localization screen (row 74) extends the genome-wide Huh atlas specifically to ~400 lipid-synthesis regulators, directly relevant to this table's existing lipidomics cluster (rows 6-8) by pinpointing which subcellular compartment each lipid-pathway enzyme natively occupies ([Mol Cell Proteomics 2005](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15716577/); Qian ref 74, discussed explicitly in the main text's spatial-organization section) — a direct prerequisite for compartment-targeted metabolic engineering (e.g., relocalizing terpenoid pathway enzymes to the ER or peroxisome for flux channeling). Yue et al.'s 2017 comparative genome-structure study of domesticated and wild yeasts (row 75) supplies structural-variation and pangenome context complementary to the natural-variation cluster already in this table (Peter 2018 row 65, baseline Loegler 2025 and Caudal 2024) ([Nat Genet 2017](https://doi.org/10.1038/ng.3847), PMID [28416820](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28416820/); Qian ref 122), helping distinguish domestication-associated structural changes — plausible engineering targets — from lineage-specific structural noise.

A related but structurally distinct addition, Galardini et al.'s 2019 four-strain-background chemogenomic comparison (row 73), was uncovered during the exhaustive reference-list pass as a genuine prior-pass miss rather than a previously logged exclusion: it directly quantifies how much a gene-deletion phenotype changes between genetic backgrounds (3,786 genes × 4 strains × 38 conditions, ~18.5% of phenotypes changing on average), with data fully released on GEO (**GSE123118**) and a reproducible GitHub pipeline ([Mol Syst Biol 2019](https://doi.org/10.15252/msb.20198831), PMID [31885205](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31885205/); Qian ref 48). This is the only dataset in the entire table directly addressing genetic-background transferability — a first-order risk factor for whether a fitness or titer effect discovered in a laboratory reference strain will reproduce in an industrial production-strain background — and its unusually complete GEO-plus-GitHub data release places it among the most machine-readable entries added in this pass.

### Part 2 — the high-dimensional (multi-omic) chemogenomic gap

**No dataset pairs a genome-wide drug/inhibitor panel with a genome-wide omic (expression/proteome/metabolome) readout on the YKO genotype axis** — this is a genuine gap, not a search miss. Closest analogs:

- **Nadal-Ribelles 2025 (row 52)** — pooled scRNA-seq × ~3,500 KOs × osmotic/salt stress; the strongest *template* for a future "chemogenomic + omics" dataset (osmotic stress is directly relevant to high-gravity fermentation). GEO accession still to verify.
- **Turco 2023 (row 79)** — its expression layer is a *static* Kemmeren join under standard growth, not jointly measured with the chemical conditions → not a true joint readout.
- **Messner 2021 (row 57)** / **Leutert 2023 (row 56)** — rich omic × compound/stress readouts but **WT only, no deletion axis**.
- **Zelezniak 2018** (baseline) — proteome + metabolome × ~97 kinase KOs, but standard conditions, no compound panel.

Parallel gap: no genuine **eQTL/pQTL × compound** crossover (natural-variation panel with expression measured under chemical stress) exists either.
